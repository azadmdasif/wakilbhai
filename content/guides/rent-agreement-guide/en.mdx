## The two kinds of rent agreement

Almost every residential tenancy in India is documented one of two ways:

1. **11-month agreement (unregistered).** Written on stamp paper, signed by both parties and two witnesses, usually notarised. Because it is under 12 months, registration is not compulsory under Section 17 of the Registration Act, 1908. This is the standard for most house rentals.
2. **Registered lease (12 months or more).** Compulsorily registered at the sub-registrar's office, with stamp duty and registration fee paid on the lease value. Used for long-term residential leases and most commercial premises.

A third pattern — the **leave and licence agreement** — is dominant in Maharashtra. Legally it grants permission to occupy rather than a lease interest, and Maharashtra requires it to be **registered whatever the duration**, with a convenient online e-registration system.

## Why everyone uses 11 months

Registration means stamp duty on the rent value, a registration fee, biometric attendance at the sub-registrar's office, and time. Keeping the term at 11 months avoids the mandatory-registration threshold, so the agreement can be executed the same day on stamp paper. The trade-offs:

- An unregistered agreement **cannot by itself prove a lease longer than its term** — fine for genuine short tenancies, renewed each year.
- In a serious dispute, a registered document has more evidentiary weight; an unregistered one is still admissible for a tenancy up to 11 months if properly stamped.
- Some banks, visa offices and government processes prefer registered agreements as address proof. {/* VERIFY: current acceptance practices */}

## What the agreement must cover

A good rent agreement is boring and specific. The essential clauses:

- **Parties and property** — full names, parents' names, permanent addresses; exact address of the premises
- **Term and start date** — 11 months, with the renewal understanding stated
- **Rent** — amount, due date, payment mode, and the escalation on renewal (5–10% is customary)
- **Security deposit** — amount, that it is interest-free (or not), the refund timeline after vacating, and what may be deducted
- **Notice period** — typically 1–2 months on either side, and any lock-in period
- **Maintenance and repairs** — who pays society charges, minor vs major repairs
- **Utilities** — electricity, water, gas, internet transfers and bill responsibility
- **Use restrictions** — residential use only, subletting prohibition, guest policy if any
- **Landlord's entry** — inspection with reasonable prior notice
- **Termination events** — non-payment, breach, and the handover condition

Annex an **inventory list with photos** if the house is furnished — it is the single best deposit-dispute prevention there is.

In practice, many 11-month agreements in northern states are made on ₹100 stamp paper by convention — but if the applicable duty is higher, an under-stamped agreement can attract a penalty when produced in court. Buying e-stamp paper of the correct value costs little compared to that risk.

## Step-by-step: making the agreement

1. **Agree the terms** — use the checklist above; don't leave deposit refund and notice period to memory.
2. **Get it drafted** — from a template for straightforward cases (our free format below), or professionally drafted (₹199 with WakilBhai) when there are non-standard terms like lock-ins, company leases or shared houses.
3. **Buy stamp paper / e-stamp** of the correct value in the landlord's or tenant's name.
4. **Print and sign** — both parties sign every page, with **two witnesses** signing at the end with names and addresses.
5. **Notarise** (customary for 11-month agreements) or **register** at the sub-registrar's office if the term is 12+ months or your state requires it — both parties attend with ID, photos, and property proof.
6. **Keep counterparts** — one signed original each, plus scans shared on WhatsApp/email so the date is provable.
