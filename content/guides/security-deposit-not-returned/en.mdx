## First, understand your position

The security deposit is **your money held in trust** against unpaid dues and damage. Once you vacate and hand over the keys, the landlord's right is limited to making **legitimate, provable deductions** — unpaid rent, unpaid utility bills, and repair of damage beyond normal wear and tear. Everything else must come back to you.

Your position is strongest when you have:

- The **rent agreement** mentioning the deposit amount and refund terms
- **Proof of payment** of the deposit (bank transfer, receipt, even old messages)
- **Rent and bill payment records** for your tenancy
- **Photos or a video of the flat at handover**, ideally with the landlord present or acknowledged on WhatsApp
- Written communication where the landlord acknowledges the deposit or promises to return it

Even if you paid the deposit in cash without a receipt, messages and call recordings acknowledging it can establish the claim — it just takes more work.

## The typical recovery process

### Step 1 — Written demand with a deadline

Send the landlord a polite but firm written request — WhatsApp plus email is fine — stating the deposit amount, the handover date, and a clear deadline (7–15 days is common) to refund it. Ask them to share any deductions with bills or proof. This message often resolves the matter, and if it doesn't, it becomes evidence that you asked and they refused or ignored it.

### Step 2 — Advocate's legal notice

If the deadline lapses, the next step is a **formal legal notice sent by an advocate**. The notice typically:

- Sets out the tenancy, deposit and vacating facts with dates
- Rejects unjustified deductions and demands the amount with a final deadline (usually 15 days)
- States the legal action that follows — a civil recovery suit, interest, and costs
- Is sent by registered post with acknowledgement, keeping proof

This is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost step in the whole process. For a landlord, defending a court case over one deposit is rarely worth it, so a large share of disputes settle here.

## What landlords commonly deduct — and what's actually fair

| Deduction claimed | Usually justified? |
| --- | --- |
| Unpaid rent or utility bills | Yes, with statements or bills |
| Repainting after a multi-year tenancy | Often **not** — normal wear and tear; check what the agreement says |
| Deep cleaning charges | Only if the agreement provides for it or the flat was left in poor condition |
| Broken fittings, damaged furniture | Yes, if beyond normal use and provable (photos, inventory list) |
| "Notice period" rent when you gave proper notice | No — if you complied with the agreement's notice clause |

If the agreement is silent, reasonableness decides. Keep the argument factual: dates, amounts, photos, and the agreement's clauses.

## How to avoid this problem next time

- Insist the deposit and refund timeline are **written into the agreement**, including the deduction rules.
- Pay the deposit **by bank transfer**, never untraceable cash.
- Do a **move-in and move-out inventory** with photos shared on WhatsApp so the record is timestamped.
- Reconcile dues a month before vacating and get the landlord's confirmation in writing.
